Stakeholder Management: The Foundation

Definition: Stakeholder management is the process of maintaining good relationships with the people who have the most influence on your work.

Why It's Critical

  • Encourages open communication and teamwork
  • Builds trust among project members
  • Prevents misalignment on project goals
  • Reduces conflicts and surprises during execution
Key Principle
Without effective stakeholder management, even technically sound projects are less likely to succeed. People problems sink more projects than technical problems.

Understanding Stakeholder Motivations

Not all resistance comes from the same place. Identify the underlying driver:

💰 Budgetary Motivations

Concern about costs, ROI, resource allocation, or financial constraints

Example: CFO pushing back on adding features that increase project costs

🔥 Interpersonal Motivations

Worry about team dynamics, workload, training needs, or change resistance

Example: Department head concerned about disrupting team workflow

👯 Career-Focused Motivations

Personal goals: maintaining position, seeking promotion, building reputation

Example: Manager wanting visible project success to advance their career

Preparing for Stakeholder Negotiations

Before You Enter the Conversation

  • Complete your stakeholder analysis: Know who has power and interest
  • Identify motivations: What's driving their position?
  • Understand the project constraints: What's negotiable vs. fixed?
  • Gather your evidence: Data, benchmarks, and examples ready
  • Define your desired outcome: What does success look like?

Influence: Your Primary Tool as PM

Reality Check: As a project manager, you rarely have direct authority over all stakeholders and resources. Your ability to influence becomes your primary tool for success.

Why Influence Matters

  • You need buy-in from people you don't manage
  • Decisions require consensus across departments
  • Resources are controlled by other managers
  • Stakeholders have competing priorities
Key Principle
Influence without authority is a core competency for project managers. This is why soft skills often matter more than technical expertise.

Step 1: Establish Credibility

People listen to those they trust and respect. Build your credibility through:

Professional Expertise

  • Demonstrate project management knowledge
  • Reference industry standards
  • Show understanding of the domain

Track Record

  • Reference past project successes
  • Share relevant experience
  • Acknowledge lessons learned

Thorough Preparation

  • Come with detailed analysis
  • Anticipate questions
  • Have data readily available

⚠️ Avoid These

  • ✗ Being unprepared
  • ✗ Making promises you can't keep
  • ✗ Dismissing valid concerns
Slide 14 of 41

Step 2: Frame for Common Ground

Start by emphasizing shared goals before addressing disagreements.

Psychology Principle: People are more receptive to compromise after being reminded of common ground. It shifts from "you vs. me" to "us vs. the problem."

Effective Opening Statements

"We're all here because we want to deliver a solution that improves customer experience and operational efficiency."

"I know we all share the goal of completing this project on time and within budget."

"Let's start by acknowledging what we agree on: quality and user satisfaction are our top priorities."

Negotiation Technique: Listen First

The Principle

Hear stakeholder concerns completely before presenting your position.

Implementation:

  • ✓ Ask open-ended questions
  • ✓ Use active listening
  • ✓ Take visible notes
  • ✓ Resist immediate rebuttals

Why It Works

When people feel heard, they become more open to hearing your perspective.

You may learn information that allows you to reframe your proposal more effectively.

Important Note
"Listening first" doesn't mean agreeing with everything. It means understanding the full landscape before proposing solutions.
Slide 16 of 41

Step 3: Provide Evidence

Support your position with concrete data and examples.

📊 Data-Driven Decision Making

Use metrics, statistics, and quantitative analysis to demonstrate impact and feasibility.

🎯 Industry Benchmarks

Reference standards and best practices from your industry or similar organizations.

💼 Concrete Examples

Share case studies from similar projects or documented outcomes from past experiences.

Pro Tip
Have your evidence organized and ready. Nothing undermines credibility faster than fumbling for data during a negotiation.
Slide 17 of 41

Step 4: Connect Emotionally

Balance Required: Emotional connection builds trust and understanding, but must be balanced with professional boundaries and objective decision-making.

How to Connect

  • Acknowledge valid concerns genuinely
  • Show empathy for their pressures
  • Build relationships beyond transactions
  • Demonstrate understanding of their perspective
  • Use inclusive language ("we," "our team")

Example Approach

"I understand the pressure you're under to reduce costs. As someone managing this project, I share your concern about budget. Let's work together to find a solution that protects both quality and costs."

⚠️ Avoid

Manipulation, false empathy, or overly personal relationships that could compromise objectivity.

Technique: Ask Questions to Define Measurable Goals

✗ The Problem

Vague stakeholder requests:

  • "Make it better"
  • "Faster service"
  • "Improve the experience"
  • "Increase efficiency"

✓ The Solution

Targeted questions:

  • "What metrics would show success?"
  • "Can you give me an example?"
  • "How would we know we've met this goal?"
  • "What would 'better' look like?"

Result: "We want to reduce average customer checkout time by 30% within the first six weeks of tablet implementation" (Specific, Measurable, Time-bound)

Slide 19 of 41

Technique: Explain the "Why" Before the "What"

Persuasion sequence matters: reasoning first, request second.

✗ Ineffective Approach

"We need to keep the scope limited to two restaurant locations and not expand to three."

Why it fails: Stakeholder evaluates the request before understanding the reasoning.

✓ Effective Approach

"Based on our timeline analysis and resource availability, limiting the pilot to two locations allows us to ensure thorough staff training and gather high-quality data. With three locations, we'd spread our team too thin. For these reasons, I recommend we stick with two locations."

Why it works: If they agree with reasoning, they're more likely to accept the conclusion.

The Principle of Mutual Benefit

Definition: Mutual benefit is when all parties involved gain some kind of benefit or advantage. The goal is win-win, not win-lose.

Example Scenario

Disagreement: You want to hire 5 people for the project. Stakeholder wants to limit headcount to 3.

Mutually Beneficial Solutions

Option 1: Install automated software to handle some of the work, allowing for smaller team size while maintaining output.

Option 2: Adjust timeline or expectations so fewer people can accomplish goals without being overworked.

Remember
Reach solutions that maximize benefits, minimize losses, and are fair for all. Brainstorm multiple options that meet this criteria.

Coalition Building: Amplifying Your Influence

Definition: A coalition is a temporary alliance or partnering of individuals or groups to achieve a common purpose. When multiple people advocate together, they exert more influence than acting alone.

Why Coalitions Matter for PMs

  • Leverage credibility and influence of others
  • Demonstrate broader support for your position
  • Access diverse expertise and perspectives
  • Build relationships across organizational boundaries
Key Insight
As a PM, you often need buy-in but lack formal authority. Coalition-building lets you influence through collective advocacy rather than individual persuasion.
Slide 22 of 41

Strategic Coalition Member Selection

Not everyone should be in your coalition. Select strategically:

Selection Criteria

  • Expertise Alignment: Relevant knowledge
  • Relationship Capital: Connections with decision-makers
  • Vested Interest: Benefits from your outcome
  • Organizational Influence: Support carries weight

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • ✗ Only recruiting "yes people"
  • ✗ Building coalitions that are too large
  • ✗ Hiding your role in organizing
  • ✗ Not briefing coalition members

Remember: Quality over quantity. 3-4 well-chosen allies are better than 10 marginal supporters.

The Triple Constraint Model (Iron Triangle)

Every project operates within three interconnected constraints:

SCOPE
What we deliver
TIME
When we deliver
BUDGET
What it costs
QUALITY
Critical Principle
Changing one constraint affects the others. Want more scope? Expect increased time or budget. Need faster delivery? Reduce scope or increase resources.
Slide 24 of 41

Managing Scope Change Requests

When scope changes arrive (and they will), follow this framework:

Step 1: Acknowledge & Document

"Thank you for this idea. Let me work with the team to understand the full implications for timeline, budget, and resources so we can make an informed decision together."

Step 2: Analyze Using Triple Constraint

Calculate impacts on timeline (hours/days), budget (cost estimates), resources (people/expertise), and quality/risk (new challenges)

Step 3: Present Options with Trade-offs

Offer multiple alternatives with clear implications for each choice

Slide 25 of 41

Saying "No" Professionally

Sometimes the right answer is declining a request. Here's how to do it while maintaining relationships:

The Professional "No" Structure

  1. Acknowledge the merit of their idea or concern
  2. Provide context about constraints and impacts
  3. State your recommendation clearly and directly
  4. Explain the reasoning behind your position
  5. Offer alternatives when possible
  6. Invite discussion to ensure understanding
Remember
Saying "no" professionally and with solid reasoning often earns more respect than always saying "yes."

Key Takeaways

  • Stakeholder management is continuous, not a one-time activity. Relationships need ongoing attention throughout the project lifecycle.
  • Influence without authority is a core PM competency. Master the four-step framework: credibility, common ground, evidence, emotional connection.
  • Preparation is 80% of negotiation success. Use stakeholder analysis, understand motivations, and gather evidence before conversations.
  • Seek mutual benefit in all negotiations. Win-win solutions preserve relationships and lead to better outcomes.
  • Coalition building amplifies your influence. Strategic partnerships help you achieve goals you couldn't accomplish alone.
  • The triple constraint is real. Changes to scope, time, or budget always affect the others. Make trade-offs explicit.
  • Documentation protects everyone. Record decisions, changes, and rationale with timestamps.

Connection to Stakeholder Management
SMART goals transform vague stakeholder requests into clear, negotiable objectives. They provide the foundation for project charters and scope agreements.
Slide 29 of 41

SMART Goals Breakdown - Part 1

S - Specific

Clear and detailed, answering key questions:

  • • What am I aiming to accomplish?
  • • Who is involved?
  • • Where will this take place?
  • • What are the requirements and constraints?

Tip: Look for subjective words like "bigger," "better," "faster" and replace them with concrete terms.

M - Measurable

Quantifiable metrics and success criteria:

  • • How much? How many?
  • • How will I know when it's accomplished?
  • • What indicators will show progress?

Tip: Use benchmarking—research how others in your industry measure success.

A - Attainable

Challenging but realistic given resources:

  • • Can it realistically be done?
  • • Do we have the time, resources, and people?
  • • What constraints might prevent achievement?
Slide 30 of 41

SMART Goals Breakdown - Part 2

R - Relevant

Aligns with broader organizational goals:

  • • Does this make sense for us as a company or project team?
  • • Does it align with other efforts and priorities?
  • • Is this the right time?
  • • Does it match organizational OKRs or strategic objectives?

Example: If your company's objective is "provide quick meals for working families," a relevant project goal might be "decrease customer checkout time by 10% in 6 weeks."

T - Time-bound

Clear deadlines and milestones:

  • • When should this be completed?
  • • What can I accomplish in 6 months? 6 weeks?
  • • What milestones mark progress?

Remember: Adding a deadline creates urgency and helps you track whether the goal is achievable within project constraints.

Benchmarking & Industry Metrics

Benchmarking: Evaluating success against industry standards. When struggling to make goals measurable, research how others in your field quantify success.

Industry-Specific Success Metrics

🍽️ Restaurant Industry

  • • Table turnover rate: Average time guests spend at table
  • • Prime cost: Labor cost + total cost of goods (food/drink)
  • • Average check amount: Average spend per guest meal

💻 Tech Industry

  • • Sprint velocity: Story points completed per sprint
  • • Deployment frequency: How often code is deployed
  • • Mean time to recovery: Time to restore service after incident

🛍️ Retail Industry

  • • Conversion rate: Percentage of visitors who purchase
  • • Average transaction value: Average purchase amount
  • • Customer lifetime value: Total revenue per customer

SMART Goals in Stakeholder Negotiations

How SMART Goals Strengthen Negotiations

  • Prevent scope creep: Measurable criteria make it clear when requests fall outside agreed-upon goals
  • Align expectations: Everyone knows exactly what success looks like
  • Create objective criteria: Removes subjectivity from project assessment
  • Support project charters: SMART goals form the foundation of formal project documentation
  • Enable productive negotiations: Concrete goals make trade-offs discussable
Negotiation in Action
Stakeholder says: "I want this to be better."
PM responds: "Let's define what 'better' means in measurable terms. What metrics would show us we've succeeded?"
Result: SMART goal both parties can agree on and track objectively.

Defining Project Scope

Project Scope: The boundaries of a project that define what is included and excluded from the work. Scope establishes clear expectations and prevents misalignment.

Why Scope Matters

✓¦ Clear Scope Provides:

  • Shared understanding among stakeholders
  • Framework for decision-making
  • Protection against scope creep
  • Basis for timeline and budget estimates

⚠️ Without Clear Scope:

  • Conflicting stakeholder expectations
  • Continuous scope creep
  • Budget and timeline overruns
  • Team frustration and confusion
Connection to Stakeholder Negotiations
Well-defined scope gives you objective criteria when stakeholders request changes. Instead of subjective discussions, you reference agreed-upon boundaries.
Slide 35 of 41

In-Scope vs. Out-of-Scope

Determining what's included requires strategic thinking and stakeholder alignment.

Critical Questions to Determine Scope

✓¦ What's needed to achieve the project goal?

Only include deliverables and tasks that directly contribute to accomplishing SMART goals.

💬 Which project details are stakeholders aligned on?

Use stakeholder analysis to identify areas of agreement and potential conflict.

🔍 Do stakeholders disagree on any elements?

Address disagreements early—don't leave ambiguity that creates problems later.

🔧 What should be explicitly out-of-scope?

Document what you're NOT doing to prevent future misunderstandings.

Example: Sauce & Spoon Tablet Rollout Scope

✓¦ IN-SCOPE

  • • Tablet Installation: Two pilot locations
  • • Staff Training: Initial training sessions for pilot restaurant employees
  • • Technical Support: Implementation team available during pilot phase
  • • Performance Metrics: Track order time, sales data, customer satisfaction
  • • Timeline: Six-week pilot period

✗ OUT-OF-SCOPE

  • • Full Rollout: Expanding to all restaurant locations
  • • Custom Development: Building new software features
  • • Hardware Upgrades: Kitchen equipment or POS system changes
  • • Marketing Campaign: Customer-facing promotions about tablets
  • • Menu Redesign: Updating menu items or pricing
Pro Tip
Notice how out-of-scope items aren't "bad ideas"—they're just not part of THIS project. Clearly documenting them prevents stakeholders from assuming they're included.

Identifying Project Benefits

Benefits: The expected gains of a project. These can be direct (monetary) or indirect (engagement, satisfaction, efficiency). Benefits must outweigh costs to justify project investment.

💵 Direct Benefits

Tangible, measurable financial gains:

  • • Increased revenue/sales
  • • Reduced operating costs
  • • Time savings (measured in dollars)
  • • Efficiency improvements with ROI

Sauce & Spoon Example:

"Increase sales by estimated 10% through faster table turnover and reduced order errors"

📝 Indirect Benefits

Important but less quantifiable gains:

  • • Improved customer satisfaction
  • • Enhanced brand reputation
  • • Better employee morale
  • • Competitive advantage

Sauce & Spoon Example:

"Provide clear data on ordering patterns to guide future menu and operational decisions"

Connection to Stakeholder Negotiations
Emphasize benefits that matter most to specific stakeholders. CFO? Lead with direct financial benefits. Operations manager? Highlight efficiency and data insights.

Scope Negotiation in Practice

Using your project charter and scope definition to navigate difficult conversations.

⚠ ️ Common Scope Challenges

  • Scope Creep: Gradual expansion of project scope without adjusting timeline/budget
  • Gold Plating: Adding unnecessary features stakeholders didn't request
  • Ambiguous Requirements: Unclear deliverables that allow different interpretations
  • Competing Priorities: Stakeholders with conflicting views on what's most important

Negotiation Techniques for Scope Discussions

1. Reference the Charter

"Let's look at our agreed-upon scope in the charter. This request would add [X] to our deliverables."

2. Present Trade-Offs

"To add this feature, we'd need to extend the timeline by 3 weeks OR remove [other deliverable]."

3. Acknowledge Value

"That's a valuable idea. Let's evaluate it against our project goals to see if it fits this phase."

4. Suggest Alternatives

"While that's out-of-scope for the pilot, we could document it for Phase 2 consideration."

5. Use Data

"Based on our resource allocation, adding this would require an additional $15K and 120 hours."

6. Escalate When Needed

"This is a significant scope change. Should we schedule a meeting with the project sponsor to decide?"

Documenting Scope Decisions

Critical Practice: Every scope discussion, decision, and change must be documented with timestamps and stakeholder agreement. Documentation protects everyone and prevents future disputes.

What to Document

📝 Scope Discussions

  • • Date and participants
  • • Topic/request discussed
  • • Options presented
  • • Decision reached

📊 Scope Changes

  • • What changed (before/after)
  • • Why the change was needed
  • • Impact on timeline/budget
  • • Stakeholder approvals

✓¦ Agreements

  • • What was agreed upon
  • • Who agreed (names/roles)
  • • When agreement was reached
  • • Next steps/action items

🔍 Declined Requests

  • • What was requested
  • • Why it was declined
  • • Alternative solutions offered
  • • Stakeholder understanding
Pro Tip: Communication Format
After scope discussions, send a follow-up email summarizing the conversation and decision. Ask stakeholders to confirm their agreement in writing. This creates a paper trail that protects both you and the stakeholders.
